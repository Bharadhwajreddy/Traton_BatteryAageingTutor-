3:T442,Nobody wants to wait ten years, so campaigns accelerate: raise temperature, cycle continuously, deepen DoD. Two failure modes hide here. First, **extrapolating from 45–60 °C down to 25 °C assumes the activation energy is constant** across that span — Arrhenius is a model, not a law of nature, and side reactions with different Ea values change their relative importance with temperature, so the effective Ea you fit hot is not the one that governs cool. Second, and worse: **acceleration can change the dominant mechanism entirely**. At 60 °C, SEI growth and [[electrolyte oxidation]] dominate and [[lithium plating]] is essentially impossible; at 5 °C the ranking inverts. Back-to-back cycling also suppresses calendar-like relaxation effects and keeps the cell permanently warm. Push acceleration hard enough and you are no longer ageing your battery faster — **you are ageing a different battery**. The defence: accelerate modestly, span the mechanism-relevant range rather than maximising stress, and validate at least one condition at realistic stress even though it's slow.4:T4b9,The pipeline, in order:
- **Per-condition fits**: for each matrix cell, fit Q_loss = k·t^z (calendar) or k·FEC^z (cycle). Decide early whether z is shared across conditions or free per condition — shared z is the standard and far better identified.
- **Stress-factor regression**: the per-condition rate constants k become the dependent variable; regress them on T (Arrhenius → Ea), SoC, DoD, C-rate to obtain the factor functions. Plot ln(k) vs 1/T and *look* at it before trusting any Ea.
- **Cycle/calendar separation**: subtract the calendar contribution from cycle-test data (equation below) — skipping this inflates cycle parameters with calendar fade.
- **Validation on held-out *dynamic* profiles** — the step that separates a fit from a model. The matrix is constant-condition by construction; reality is not. Run cells (or at minimum, simulations against independent data) on synthetic truck duty cycles — [[depot charging]], delivery legs, [[opportunity charging]] spikes, weekend rests — and check prediction error there. A model can interpolate its own training matrix beautifully and still fail dynamics, because superposition and path-independence were never tested by the matrix.5:T40c,A model can be well-posed and still unidentifiable from your matrix. The classic traps: **T–SoC correlation** — if the calendar grid is sparse or unbalanced (say, the 55 °C leg only ran at high SoC), the fit can trade activation energy against SoC-factor steepness almost freely; both parameter sets reproduce the matrix, and they extrapolate very differently. **Rate–exponent trade-off** — over short campaigns, a higher k with lower z fits nearly as well as the reverse, and the difference only explodes at year eight, exactly where you need the model. **Factor leakage** — anything the structure can't represent (a real T·DoD interaction, a missing mechanism) gets absorbed into whichever factor is most flexible, corrupting its interpretation. Nominally identical cells diverge through manufacturing spread (coating thickness, electrolyte fill, formation differences), and the divergence typically *grows* with age because degradation is self-amplifying: a slightly weaker cell runs at slightly higher relative current and temperature, ageing faster still. For pack life this changes the question entirely: a series string is limited by its **weakest cell**, so pack lifetime is a statement about the *minimum* of N samples, not the mean. With hundreds of cells in series, the expected weakest cell sits far into the distribution's tail — extreme-value statistics territory — and a 2 % standard deviation in cell life can shave a disproportionate slice off pack life.
