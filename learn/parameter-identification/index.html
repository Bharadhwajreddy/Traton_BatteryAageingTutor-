Tests & Parameter Identification

Where model parameters actually come from: calendar and cycle test matrices, reference performance tests, accelerated-ageing pitfalls, fitting workflow, identifiability traps, and uncertainty propagation.

You cannot download a model

A semi-empirical model is an empty skeleton until someone pays for the data that fills it. Published parameter sets are fits to one cell type, format, and electrolyte generation, tested over one window of conditions — they transfer to your cell roughly as well as another person's glasses prescription. So before trusting any ageing prediction, ask the only question that matters: what test campaign produced these parameters, and does my use case live inside it? This section is about how such campaigns are designed, why they take one to two years, and how raw fade curves become the stress factors and exponents you met in the model-families section.

The calendar matrix: T × SoC × patience

Calendar parameters come from storing cells at controlled temperature and SoC and measuring them periodically. A typical design is a 3×4 grid — three temperatures (say 25/40/55 °C) crossed with four SoC levels (say 20/50/80/100 %) — held for 6–24 months. The design logic falls straight out of the model structure: you need ≥3 temperatures to fit an Arrhenius activation energy (two points give you a line through anything), enough SoC levels to resolve the nonlinear f_SoC shape including its high-SoC upturn, and enough time points per condition to fit the exponent z_cal — distinguishing √t from t^0.7 on six months of data is statistically brutal, which is why short campaigns produce confident, wrong exponents. Cells are checked under identical reference conditions at regular intervals; between checks they just sit there, expensively.

The cycle matrix and the factorial explosion

Cycle ageing has at least four knobs: DoD, charge/discharge C-rate, temperature, and mean SoC of the cycling window. A full factorial at modest resolution — 4 DoD × 3 C-rate × 3 T × 3 mean-SoC — is 108 conditions, times ≥3 cells each for cell-to-cell variation, times months per condition: nobody funds that. Real campaigns use fractional designs: a one-factor-at-a-time star around a reference condition, or formal design-of-experiments layouts, accepting that interaction terms (does DoD stress depend on temperature?) are partially confounded. This is a structural honesty point: when a model multiplies independent stress factors, that separability was usually imposed by the test design, not discovered in the data — the campaign never contained the combinations that could refute it.

Reference performance tests (RPT): the measurement heartbeat

Ageing itself happens at the matrix conditions, but measurement must happen at one fixed, standard condition — otherwise temperature and SoC effects on the measurement masquerade as ageing. Every few weeks, each cell gets an RPT: a full capacity check at standard temperature and low rate; resistance pulses (current steps at fixed SoC, evaluating ΔV/ΔI at 1 s/10 s) for power-based SoH; often EIS to separate ohmic, charge-transfer, and diffusion impedance; and sometimes a C/20 charge–discharge for DVA/ICA, which is the cheapest non-destructive way to attribute fade to LLI versus LAM. Two campaign-design tensions: RPTs interrupt and perturb the very ageing you're observing (a C/20 full cycle from a 100 % SoC calendar cell is a real intervention), and every RPT costs tester-channel time — the scarce resource of any battery lab.

Accelerated ageing: the seductive trap

Nobody wants to wait ten years, so campaigns accelerate: raise temperature, cycle continuously, deepen DoD. Two failure modes hide here. First, extrapolating from 45–60 °C down to 25 °C assumes the activation energy is constant across that span — Arrhenius is a model, not a law of nature, and side reactions with different Ea values change their relative importance with temperature, so the effective Ea you fit hot is not the one that governs cool. Second, and worse: acceleration can change the dominant mechanism entirely. At 60 °C, SEI growth and electrolyte oxidation dominate and lithium plating is essentially impossible; at 5 °C the ranking inverts. Back-to-back cycling also suppresses calendar-like relaxation effects and keeps the cell permanently warm. Push acceleration hard enough and you are no longer ageing your battery faster — you are ageing a different battery. The defence: accelerate modestly, span the mechanism-relevant range rather than maximising stress, and validate at least one condition at realistic stress even though it's slow.

From fade curves to parameters: the fitting workflow

The pipeline, in order:

  • Per-condition fits: for each matrix cell, fit Q_loss = k·t^z (calendar) or k·FEC^z (cycle). Decide early whether z is shared across conditions or free per condition — shared z is the standard and far better identified.
  • Stress-factor regression: the per-condition rate constants k become the dependent variable; regress them on T (Arrhenius → Ea), SoC, DoD, C-rate to obtain the factor functions. Plot ln(k) vs 1/T and look at it before trusting any Ea.
  • Cycle/calendar separation: subtract the calendar contribution from cycle-test data (equation below) — skipping this inflates cycle parameters with calendar fade.
  • *Validation on held-out dynamic profiles* — the step that separates a fit from a model. The matrix is constant-condition by construction; reality is not. Run cells (or at minimum, simulations against independent data) on synthetic truck duty cycles — depot charging, delivery legs, opportunity charging spikes, weekend rests — and check prediction error there. A model can interpolate its own training matrix beautifully and still fail dynamics, because superposition and path-independence were never tested by the matrix.

θ collects all parameters (reference rates, activation energy, stress-factor coefficients, exponents). The double sum runs over every test condition i and every RPT measurement j within it; w_ij are weights. Two practical subtleties hide in those weights: conditions with more RPTs dominate unless you reweight, and early measurements (tiny losses, comparable to measurement noise) can drag the fitted exponent around. Also note θ enters nonlinearly (exponentials, power laws), so the optimisation landscape has local minima — always fit from multiple starting points and sanity-check that the fitted Ea and exponents land in physically plausible ranges.

Calendar contamination subtraction
Qcycisolated(FEC)  =  Qtotalmeas  −  Qcal(ttest, T, SoC‾)Q_{\mathrm{cyc}}^{\mathrm{isolated}}(FEC) \;=\; Q_{\mathrm{total}}^{\mathrm{meas}} \;-\; Q_{\mathrm{cal}}\big(t_{\mathrm{test}},\, T,\, \overline{SoC}\big)Qcycisolated​(FEC)=Qtotalmeas​−Qcal​(ttest​,T,SoC)

A cell on a cycling test also ages calendrically the whole time — it sits at some mean SoC and temperature for every hour of the months-long test. To get clean cycle parameters, subtract the calendar loss predicted for the test duration at the test temperature and the time-averaged SoC of the cycling window. Consequences: (1) you must fit the calendar model first — campaign order is forced; (2) gentle cycle conditions can be 30–50 % calendar fade, so skipping the subtraction badly inflates cycle stress factors; (3) the subtraction itself leans on superposition, so any calendar–cycle interaction (e.g. cycling cracking the SEI) leaks into whichever term absorbs it. Honest papers state this; sloppy ones don't.

Identifiability: when the data cannot tell parameters apart

A model can be well-posed and still unidentifiable from your matrix. The classic traps: T–SoC correlation — if the calendar grid is sparse or unbalanced (say, the 55 °C leg only ran at high SoC), the fit can trade activation energy against SoC-factor steepness almost freely; both parameter sets reproduce the matrix, and they extrapolate very differently. Rate–exponent trade-off — over short campaigns, a higher k with lower z fits nearly as well as the reverse, and the difference only explodes at year eight, exactly where you need the model. Factor leakage — anything the structure can't represent (a real T·DoD interaction, a missing mechanism) gets absorbed into whichever factor is most flexible, corrupting its interpretation. Diagnostics worth running every time: parameter correlation matrices from the fit, profile-likelihood or bootstrap confidence intervals, and the simple discipline of checking whether independently-fitted parameters (Ea from calendar vs from cycle tests) agree with each other.

From parameter uncertainty to lifetime uncertainty

A fitted model without uncertainty bands is a liability, because lifetime prediction amplifies parameter uncertainty: errors in the time exponent compound over years, and errors in Ea compound exponentially with the temperature gap between test and field. The workable recipe: extract the parameter covariance from the fit (or bootstrap over cells and RPT points), then Monte-Carlo the lifetime simulation — sample parameter sets, run each through the duty-cycle simulation, and report the spread of years-to-EOL rather than a single number. Expect asymmetry: ±10 % on parameters rarely maps to ±10 % on lifetime. For decisions, report a percentile band (e.g. 10th–90th) and identify which parameter dominates the spread — that tells you what additional testing would actually buy.

🚛Truck reality
Validation profile = a week of truck life

For a heavy-duty study, the held-out validation profile should be a synthetic week, not a synthetic cycle: Monday–Friday with 1.2 FEC/day of driving including 2C gradient peaks, one MCS opportunity-charge spike midweek with the pack warm, depot AC charging each night to a chosen target SoC, and a 60-hour weekend rest at whatever SoC Friday operations left behind. If your fitted model predicts the fade of cells run on that profile within its own uncertainty band, you have a model. If you only ever checked it against 25 °C / 50 % DoD / 1C lab cycling, you have a fit wearing a model's clothes.

💡Practical tip
Getting data without a two-year lab campaign

Students have realistic options short of running their own matrix: (1) public ageing datasets — several universities and labs have released multi-year calendar and cycling sets (mostly small-format cells; check chemistry and conditions against your use case before leaning on them); (2) supplier data under NDA — OEMs and truck makers (MAN-type companies) hold cell-vendor ageing reports and warranty curves; an industrial thesis can often access them, with the constraint that you publish methods, not numbers; (3) literature-typical parameters, clearly labelled as such — assemble plausible parameters from the published range, state loudly that they are typical rather than fitted, and compensate with sensitivity analysis. That third option is exactly what this app does, which is why every tool here carries a 'trends, not sign-off' disclaimer.

⚠️Common misconception
"R² = 0.99 on the test matrix, so the model predicts field life"

A near-perfect fit to the training matrix demonstrates flexibility, not predictive power — with enough stress-factor coefficients you can fit anything, including noise. The matrix is constant-condition, mechanism-filtered, and measurement-friendly by design; field life is dynamic, path-dependent, thermally messy, and a decade long. The only evidence that counts is error on data the fit never touched, ideally of a different kind: dynamic profiles, longer horizons, other cells from other production batches. Treat training-matrix R² the way you'd treat a student who aced the exact homework problems they had already seen.
