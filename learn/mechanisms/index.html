Ageing Mechanisms

What physically degrades inside NMC and LFP cells: SEI, plating, LLI, LAM, impedance rise — and how the two chemistries differ.

The one-sentence version

Almost everything we call "ageing" is one of three bookkeeping categories: LLI (cyclable lithium gets consumed by side reactions), LAM (electrode host structure becomes unusable), and impedance rise (everything gets harder to push current through). Every physical mechanism below feeds at least one of these buckets — and a good model is, at heart, an accounting system for them.

SEI growth — the ever-present tax

The electrolyte is not thermodynamically stable at the graphite anode's low potential. In the very first cycles it decomposes on the anode surface and forms the SEI — a thin passivation film. This is necessary (without it the electrolyte would decompose continuously), but the film never seals perfectly: solvent molecules and electrons keep leaking through at a slow rate, so the SEI keeps thickening for the entire life of the cell.

  • Each increment of SEI growth consumes cyclable lithium → LLI → capacity fade.
  • The thickening film is an ionic resistance in series with intercalation → impedance rise.
  • Growth is approximately limited by transport through the existing film, which is why calendar fade so often looks like square-root-of-time: the thicker the film, the slower it grows.

Lithium plating — the dangerous one

During charging, lithium ions must diffuse into graphite particles. If you push them faster than they can intercalate — high charge C-rate, low temperature, high SoC — the anode surface potential dips below 0 V vs Li/Li⁺ and metallic lithium deposits on the particle surface instead. Part of it reacts with electrolyte and is lost forever (LLI, often with fresh SEI on top), part can grow as dendrites toward the separator. Plating is the reason truck fast-charging strategy and ageing are inseparable: it is the mechanism that turns an aggressive opportunity charging profile into a sudden knee point in the fade curve.

⚠️Common misconception
SEI growth ≠ lithium plating

Both consume lithium at the anode, but they are different physics with different signatures. SEI growth is a slow chemical side reaction that happens always, fastest at high temperature, and produces smooth √t-like fade. Plating is an electrochemical deposition that happens only under specific charging conditions (cold, fast, full), is worst at low temperature, and produces abrupt, often nonlinear fade. A model fitted only on warm, gentle-cycling data will be blind to plating — and will cheerfully mispredict the life of a fast-charged truck in a Scandinavian winter.

Active material loss (LAM)

Electrodes are porous composites of active particles, binder, and conductive carbon. Cycling makes particles breathe (volume change on (de)lithiation: a few percent for graphite and NMC), and repeated breathing causes particle cracking, loss of electrical contact, and binder fatigue — host sites that still exist chemically but are electrically orphaned. On the cathode side, NMC additionally suffers transition-metal dissolution (Mn especially) and surface phase transformations at high voltage; dissolved metals migrate to the anode and poison the SEI, coupling cathode LAM to anode LLI. Deep DoD cycles and high C-rates make all of this worse — this is the microscopic origin of the Wöhler curve-style DoD stress factors you will see in cycle ageing models.

Impedance rise, electrolyte depletion, gas

Resistance grows from several sources at once: thickening SEI, cathode surface films (CEI), loss of electronic contact (LAM side-effect), electrolyte drying-out (solvent consumed by film growth, especially after plating events), and porosity clogging by deposits. Gas generation from electrolyte decomposition causes swelling and, in pouch/prismatic formats, mechanical stress and local contact loss. For a truck, impedance rise is not cosmetic: it limits fast-charge power and gradeability at low SoC, and it increases heat generation (I²R), which feeds back into faster ageing — the electro-thermal coupling treated in the Stress Factors section.

NMC vs LFP — same mechanisms, different weights

Both chemistries share the graphite anode, so SEI growth and plating physics are common ground. The differences live mostly on the cathode and in the voltage curve:

  • NMC: higher cell voltage → stronger electrolyte oxidation at high SoC, transition-metal dissolution, cathode particle cracking (especially Ni-rich). Calendar ageing is distinctly SoC-sensitive; storing full is expensive.
  • LFP: lower, very flat voltage → mild cathode-side ageing, excellent cycle life and thermal stability. Calendar ageing is dominated almost entirely by anode-side SEI; SoC sensitivity is weaker but not zero — near 100 % SoC the graphite anode still sits at its most reactive potential, so the 'store LFP full, it doesn't care' rule of thumb is only half-true.
  • Practical twist: LFP's flat OCV makes SoC estimation hard, so fleets often charge LFP to 100 % regularly for BMS recalibration — a behaviour with real calendar-ageing cost that a good model should capture.
🚛Truck reality
Truck reality check

A long-haul tractor can sit at the depot all weekend (calendar, SoC- and T-driven), megawatt-charge during the driver's 45-minute break (plating risk + heat), and pull 2–3C peaks on gradients with a hot pack (LAM + impedance). One vehicle, one week, every mechanism on this page. This is why heavy-duty ageing models cannot get away with single-stress-factor thinking — and why this app keeps dragging every mechanism back to a truck scenario.
