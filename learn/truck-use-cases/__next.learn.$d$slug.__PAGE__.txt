3:T4d3,The cell physics is identical; the *stress distribution* is from another planet. Five structural differences:
- **Throughput**: a working truck pushes **1–2 [[FEC]] per day** through its pack; a passenger car averages 0.1–0.3. The cycle-ageing clock runs five to ten times faster.
- **Charging cadence is regulated**: mandated driver breaks (45 minutes after 4.5 hours of driving in the EU) create predictable, schedulable windows for [[opportunity charging]] — and the pressure to refill a huge pack inside that window is what [[MCS]] exists for.
- **Payload-dependent load**: power demand scales with gross weight, so the same route stresses the pack differently loaded vs empty — duty cycles are distributions, not curves.
- **Load shape**: long-haul means hours of near-constant highway power; urban duty means stop-go with heavy regenerative braking — thousands of shallow micro-cycles per day.
- **The depot**: trucks return to a known place, on a known schedule, with hours of guaranteed rest under controllable conditions. That guaranteed rest is the single biggest *optimisation lever* in the whole heavy-duty ageing story — parking SoC, charge timing, preconditioning are all software decisions made at the depot.4:T422,Four archetypes cover most of the heavy-duty world, and they stress the pack in characteristically different ways:
- **Long-haul**: 1.5–2 [[FEC]]/day, [[MCS]] opportunity charging at breaks, by design cycled in a narrow mid-SoC band (arrive at a charger around 20–30 %, leave around 70–80 %). High throughput, high charge power, hot pack on summer gradients.
- **Regional distribution**: ~1 FEC/day, [[depot charging]] only, highly predictable routes. The gentlest serious duty: moderate rates, overnight charging at ≤1C, controlled depot environment.
- **Urban / refuse**: low average speed, constant stop-go with heavy regen — the SoC trace is a dense ripple of shallow micro-cycles on top of a slow daily discharge. Modest daily FEC, but an enormous *count* of small swings, plus long PTO/compactor idling loads.
- **Coach**: seasonal and bursty — intense touring months, then weeks or months parked. Calendar ageing takes the lead role; the parking SoC and storage temperature during the off-season can matter more than the kilometres driven.5:T52c,Each archetype picks its own poison, and the poison picks the chemistry:
- **Long-haul** is throughput- and charging-power-dominated: heat and [[lithium plating]] risk at MCS stops, high cumulative FEC. It is also weight-limited, so energy density is revenue — **[[NMC]]** earns its higher ageing sensitivity through payload, paired with disciplined fast-charge management (preconditioning, derating, mid-SoC charging where plating margin is widest).
- **Regional distribution** is the **[[LFP]]** sweet spot: ~1 FEC/day for many years demands cycle life above all; charge rates are gentle, routes tolerate the energy-density penalty, and LFP's cost per kWh strengthens the [[TCO]] case. Dominant mechanism: plain accumulated SEI/[[LAM]] wear — boring, predictable, modelable.
- **Urban / refuse** lives on shallow micro-cycles, which the Wöhler logic prices very kindly per FEC — and LFP's cycle-life advantage compounds this. Watch instead for the *thermal* side (low speed = poor airflow, high ambient duty) and resistance growth, since regen acceptance degrades with [[impedance]].
- **Coach** is calendar-dominated: chemistry matters less than **storage discipline** — off-season SoC and temperature dwarf the cycling terms. An NMC coach parked all winter at 100 % SoC in a sunny yard is the textbook own-goal.6:T431,Make the trade concrete at pack level. Take pack-level specific energies of roughly 160–180 Wh/kg for current [[NMC]] systems and 110–140 Wh/kg for [[LFP]] (cell-to-pack designs are narrowing this, but a gap remains). A 500 kWh pack then weighs about 2.8–3.1 t in NMC and 3.6–4.5 t in LFP — a difference of order **0.7–1.5 t**. For a weight-limited long-haul combination running at the gross-weight cap, that difference is *directly lost payload* on every trip: a percent-level revenue penalty, every day, for the vehicle's whole life — typically larger in present value than the battery-cost saving and the cycle-life advantage combined. For a *volume-limited* parcel or urban truck that almost never hits the weight cap, the same tonnes cost nothing, and LFP's cycle life and cost win comfortably. The chemistry decision is thus not an electrochemistry question at all — it is a logistics question (does this duty bind on weight?) wearing an electrochemistry costume. The same logic explains why one fleet can rationally run both chemistries side by side.7:T442,Because trucks are parked most of their lives, *not driving* deserves the same engineering attention as driving:
- **Seasonal fleets**: coaches, agricultural and construction vehicles park for months. Off-season storage SoC and temperature are pure [[calendar ageing]] knobs — mid-SoC in a cool hall versus full in a hot yard can differ by a factor of a few in fade rate over the same months.
- **Dealer and upfitter lots**: a truck can spend 3–9 months between factory and first revenue kilometre — body-building, homologation, sales. If it waits at high SoC through a summer, it arrives at its first customer *measurably aged*, with nobody having driven it at all.
- **Delivery SoC**: cell and pack makers ship at ~30–50 % SoC deliberately — minimum-stress storage. The discipline should propagate down the chain: fleet procurement contracts that specify storage-SoC handling between factory and handover are cheap to write and directly convert into warranty margin.
The through-line: every stakeholder who *holds* the vehicle holds an ageing lever, whether they know it or not.8:T58d,Look at the SoC trace of a refuse truck: a slow daily drift downward with a dense, ragged ripple on top — accelerate (small discharge), brake (small regen charge), repeat a thousand times. Where exactly are the 'cycles' in that? There is no obvious answer, and you cannot just bin the throughput into [[FEC]], because the [[DoD]] [[stress factor]] is *nonlinear* — a thousand 0.5 %-swings and one 50 %-swing carry the same charge but wildly different fatigue cost, so the damage calculation needs to know the swing-size distribution. The standard solution is **rainflow counting**, imported intact from mechanical fatigue analysis, where engineers faced literally the same problem with irregular load histories on metal parts: the algorithm decomposes an arbitrary SoC trace into a set of closed cycles, each with its own depth and mean level, by matching turning points (the name comes from imagining rain dripping down a pagoda-roof plot of the signal). Each extracted cycle is then priced with the [[Wöhler curve]]-style DoD stress factor and a mean-SoC factor, and the damages summed. For highway profiles rainflow and naive FEC counting agree well; for regen-heavy urban profiles they can diverge substantially — and since shallow cycles are priced *kindly* by the nonlinearity, naive per-FEC accounting typically *overestimates* urban cycle ageing.
