Heavy-Duty Truck Use Cases

How truck duty cycles actually look — throughput, charging cadence, parking patterns — and what each archetype (long-haul, regional, urban, coach) implies for ageing and chemistry choice.

A truck is not a big car

The cell physics is identical; the stress distribution is from another planet. Five structural differences:

  • Throughput: a working truck pushes 1–2 FEC per day through its pack; a passenger car averages 0.1–0.3. The cycle-ageing clock runs five to ten times faster.
  • Charging cadence is regulated: mandated driver breaks (45 minutes after 4.5 hours of driving in the EU) create predictable, schedulable windows for opportunity charging — and the pressure to refill a huge pack inside that window is what MCS exists for.
  • Payload-dependent load: power demand scales with gross weight, so the same route stresses the pack differently loaded vs empty — duty cycles are distributions, not curves.
  • Load shape: long-haul means hours of near-constant highway power; urban duty means stop-go with heavy regenerative braking — thousands of shallow micro-cycles per day.
  • The depot: trucks return to a known place, on a known schedule, with hours of guaranteed rest under controllable conditions. That guaranteed rest is the single biggest optimisation lever in the whole heavy-duty ageing story — parking SoC, charge timing, preconditioning are all software decisions made at the depot.
📌Working assumption
Reference pack used throughout this app

Unless a page says otherwise, every worked example assumes a heavy-duty pack of ~500 kWh nominal, with roughly 450 kWh usable (the BMS reserves buffer at both ends), and EOL at 80 % capacity-SoH. These are deliberate simplifications: real packs in service span roughly 300–800 kWh, usable windows differ by chemistry and manufacturer, and — as the Foundations section argues — 80 % is a convention, not physics. Treat every number derived from these assumptions as illustrative of the reasoning, not as a datasheet.

Daily full equivalent cycles
FEC/day=EdailyEusable\mathrm{FEC/day} = \frac{E_{\mathrm{daily}}}{E_{\mathrm{usable}}}FEC/day=Eusable​Edaily​​

Daily energy throughput divided by usable pack energy gives full equivalent cycles per day — the truck's cycle-ageing clock speed. Worked numbers on the reference pack (450 kWh usable): a long-haul tractor driving 600 km/day at ~1.2 kWh/km consumes ~720 kWh/day → 1.6 FEC/day; a regional distribution truck at 350 km/day and ~1.2 kWh/km uses ~420 kWh → ~0.9 FEC/day; a passenger car (50 km/day, 0.2 kWh/km, 60 kWh usable) sits at ~0.17 FEC/day. Integrate over a 6-year duty: the long-haul truck accumulates ~2 900 FEC — more than most passenger cars see in their entire existence. This single ratio explains why cycle ageing, almost an afterthought in many car studies, is co-dominant in truck lifetime models.

Duty-cycle archetypes

Four archetypes cover most of the heavy-duty world, and they stress the pack in characteristically different ways:

  • Long-haul: 1.5–2 FEC/day, MCS opportunity charging at breaks, by design cycled in a narrow mid-SoC band (arrive at a charger around 20–30 %, leave around 70–80 %). High throughput, high charge power, hot pack on summer gradients.
  • Regional distribution: ~1 FEC/day, depot charging only, highly predictable routes. The gentlest serious duty: moderate rates, overnight charging at ≤1C, controlled depot environment.
  • Urban / refuse: low average speed, constant stop-go with heavy regen — the SoC trace is a dense ripple of shallow micro-cycles on top of a slow daily discharge. Modest daily FEC, but an enormous count of small swings, plus long PTO/compactor idling loads.
  • Coach: seasonal and bursty — intense touring months, then weeks or months parked. Calendar ageing takes the lead role; the parking SoC and storage temperature during the off-season can matter more than the kilometres driven.

Archetype → dominant mechanism → chemistry

Each archetype picks its own poison, and the poison picks the chemistry:

  • Long-haul is throughput- and charging-power-dominated: heat and lithium plating risk at MCS stops, high cumulative FEC. It is also weight-limited, so energy density is revenue — NMC earns its higher ageing sensitivity through payload, paired with disciplined fast-charge management (preconditioning, derating, mid-SoC charging where plating margin is widest).
  • Regional distribution is the LFP sweet spot: ~1 FEC/day for many years demands cycle life above all; charge rates are gentle, routes tolerate the energy-density penalty, and LFP's cost per kWh strengthens the TCO case. Dominant mechanism: plain accumulated SEI/LAM wear — boring, predictable, modelable.
  • Urban / refuse lives on shallow micro-cycles, which the Wöhler logic prices very kindly per FEC — and LFP's cycle-life advantage compounds this. Watch instead for the thermal side (low speed = poor airflow, high ambient duty) and resistance growth, since regen acceptance degrades with impedance.
  • Coach is calendar-dominated: chemistry matters less than storage discipline — off-season SoC and temperature dwarf the cycling terms. An NMC coach parked all winter at 100 % SoC in a sunny yard is the textbook own-goal.

Try it: Build two days with identical energy throughput: one using 2C opportunity charging in short bursts (long-haul style), one using a single 0.5 C overnight depot charge (regional style). Compare the ageing per FEC — then drop the pack temperature to 0 °C and watch the 2C profile's plating penalty open the gap much wider.

Parking is a use case

Because trucks are parked most of their lives, not driving deserves the same engineering attention as driving:

  • Seasonal fleets: coaches, agricultural and construction vehicles park for months. Off-season storage SoC and temperature are pure calendar ageing knobs — mid-SoC in a cool hall versus full in a hot yard can differ by a factor of a few in fade rate over the same months.
  • Dealer and upfitter lots: a truck can spend 3–9 months between factory and first revenue kilometre — body-building, homologation, sales. If it waits at high SoC through a summer, it arrives at its first customer measurably aged, with nobody having driven it at all.
  • Delivery SoC: cell and pack makers ship at ~30–50 % SoC deliberately — minimum-stress storage. The discipline should propagate down the chain: fleet procurement contracts that specify storage-SoC handling between factory and handover are cheap to write and directly convert into warranty margin.

The through-line: every stakeholder who holds the vehicle holds an ageing lever, whether they know it or not.

🚛Truck reality
Same weekly throughput, opposite ageing portfolios

A regional tractor and a refuse truck can both push ~5 FEC through their packs in a week — and age in almost disjoint ways. The tractor does it as five clean, moderately deep daily cycles with gentle overnight depot charging: its risk ledger is plain throughput wear, well described by textbook DoD and C-rate factors. The refuse truck does it as tens of thousands of regen micro-cycles at low speed in warm city air: its fatigue cost per FEC is tiny, but its thermal duty is worse and its resistance growth shows up first as shrinking regen acceptance — which then increases energy consumption, a feedback no cycle-count metric will ever show. Identical odometers of charge, different dominant mechanisms, different EOL modes, different optimal chemistries. Duty-cycle shape is not a detail; it is the diagnosis.

⚠️Common misconception
"EV car ageing studies transfer 1:1 to trucks"

They do not — not because the cells are different (often they aren't), but because the stress distribution is categorically different. Throughput: 1–2 FEC/day versus 0.1–0.3 — the cycle clock runs ~5–10× faster, so mechanisms that are negligible over a car's life become co-dominant. Charge power: MCS-class charging as a daily routine versus occasional DC fast charging — the plating margin is exercised constantly, not exceptionally. Rest patterns: guaranteed, schedulable depot rest at controllable SoC versus street parking at whatever SoC the day ended on — the calendar term is an optimisation variable for trucks, a random variable for cars. A car-calibrated model isn't slightly off for trucks; it is being asked about a region of stress space it never saw. Reuse the physics, re-weight everything else, and re-validate on heavy-duty profiles before believing a single lifetime number.
