3:T561,1. **Cycling accelerates the 'calendar' mechanism.** Particle breathing during cycling cracks the [[SEI]] and exposes fresh anode surface; the supposedly time-driven SEI growth then restarts on that surface at its fast early rate. The calendar term is therefore not independent of cycling history — a cell that cycles daily accrues more 'calendar-type' loss than a stored twin, even after subtracting the pure cycle damage.
2. **Path dependence.** Two profiles with identical total FEC and identical time-at-SoC histograms can age differently depending on *order*: hard cycling early (when the SEI is thin and fragile) is not equivalent to hard cycling late. Damage accumulators that only integrate instantaneous stress cannot represent this.
3. **Ageing moves the goalposts.** [[LAM]] shifts the electrode balancing, so an aged cell's anode sits closer to 0 V vs Li/Li⁺ at top of charge than the fresh cell's did — the [[lithium plating]] onset migrates into operating territory that used to be safe. The cycle-stress parameters fitted on fresh cells silently expire; this is a leading explanation for [[knee point]] behaviour and the classic failure of fresh-cell-parameterised models late in life.
None of this makes superposition useless — it makes it a *first-order* model whose error grows with cell age and with how far your duty cycle sits from the test matrix.4:T477,Separating the clocks experimentally is conceptually clean and practically fiddly. **Storage tests**: park cells across a matrix of temperatures and SoCs, interrupt them every few weeks for a *check-up* (a reference capacity measurement at standard conditions, often plus [[EIS]] or a slow cycle for [[DVA]]) — this isolates the calendar term. **Cycling tests**: cycle cells continuously under controlled DoD/C-rate/T with the same periodic check-ups. The subtlety everyone trips over: **a cycling test still contains calendar ageing** — the cell spends real months at some temperature and some average SoC while cycling, so the raw fade is calendar *plus* cycle. The cycle term is obtained by *subtracting a calendar prediction* from the measured total — which means your cycle parameters inherit every error in your calendar model, and the superposition assumption is baked into the dataset before any fitting begins.
