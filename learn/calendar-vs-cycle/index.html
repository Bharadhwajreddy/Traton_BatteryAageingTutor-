Calendar vs Cycle Ageing

The two clocks a battery ages by — time at rest vs charge throughput — why trucks care enormously about the resting clock, and where the standard 'just add them up' assumption breaks.

Two clocks

A battery ages on two clocks simultaneously. calendar ageing ticks with time: it happens while the cell rests, driven by temperature and storage SoC, with zero current flowing. cycle ageing ticks with throughput: it accumulates with every amp-hour pushed through, driven by DoD, C-rate, temperature, and where in the SoC range the cycling happens. The mechanisms behind each clock are mostly the ones from the Mechanisms section wearing different hats — calendar ageing is dominated by SEI growth (plus electrolyte oxidation on NMC at high SoC); cycle ageing adds the mechanical family (LAM, particle cracking) and the charging-specific one (lithium plating). Any lifetime estimate that tracks only one clock is wrong by construction.

Trucks are parked machines

Run the numbers on even a hard-used long-haul tractor: ~9 driving hours per day, ~250 operating days per year is about 2 250 hours of driving out of 8 760 hours in a year — roughly a quarter. Add charging time and the vehicle is still parked, doing nothing, for well over 70–80 % of its life; regional and seasonal vehicles park even more. This flips the intuition imported from consumer electronics: for a heavy truck, the conditions under which it does nothing — at what SoC it waits, at what temperature, for how long — are first-order lifetime determinants, not a footnote. The good news is that parking conditions are also the cheapest lever a fleet has: changing the SoC at which trucks wait out the weekend costs software, not hardware.

Calendar drivers and the √t signature

Calendar ageing has two knobs: temperature and storage SoC. Temperature acts through Arrhenius scaling of the parasitic reaction rates. Storage SoC acts mainly through the anode potential: at high SoC the graphite sits near 0 V vs Li/Li⁺, maximising the driving force for electrolyte reduction and SEI growth; NMC adds a cathode-side penalty (electrolyte oxidation at high cell voltage), making its SoC sensitivity steeper than LFP's. The time signature is the famous square-root of time: because the SEI throttles its own growth (the film is the transport bottleneck), capacity loss decelerates — fast in year one, slower every year after. Both NMC and LFP show exponents close to 0.5 in storage tests, because they share the graphite anode where the mechanism lives.

Semi-empirical calendar ageing law
Qloss,cal=kcal(T,SoC)⋅tz,z≈0.5Q_{\mathrm{loss,cal}} = k_{\mathrm{cal}}(T, \mathrm{SoC}) \cdot t^{z}, \qquad z \approx 0.5Qloss,cal​=kcal​(T,SoC)⋅tz,z≈0.5

Capacity loss from calendar ageing equals a stress-dependent rate prefactor k_cal times time raised to an exponent z. The prefactor bundles the temperature dependence (Arrhenius form) and the storage-SoC dependence (steep and fairly smooth for NMC; weaker and plateau-structured for LFP — see the Stress Factors section). The exponent z ≈ 0.5 is the fingerprint of self-limiting SEI growth and holds remarkably well across both chemistries; Naumann-style LFP calendar models and their NMC counterparts share exactly this structure and differ mainly in the fitted prefactor. Beware the seductive flattening: √t means early test data always looks better when extrapolated than the underlying truth — half the battles in lifetime testing are about how long you must test before trusting z.

Try it: Park a virtual pack for 5 years. First compare 50 % vs 100 % storage SoC at 25 °C — note the gap. Then repeat at 35 °C and watch the gap grow. Finally switch chemistry: see how much flatter LFP's SoC sensitivity is than NMC's, and how the √t shape makes year 1 the most expensive year in every scenario.

Cycle drivers and the throughput clock

Cycle ageing is accounted against charge throughput, normalised as FEC — full equivalent cycles. The rate per FEC is scaled by stress factors: deeper DoD (more particle breathing → fatigue and cracking), higher charge C-rate (heat, concentration gradients, plating risk), temperature (with its U-shaped twist — see Stress Factors), and the mean SoC of the cycling window (cycling near the top keeps the anode in its reactive zone the whole time). The throughput exponent is typically closer to linear than calendar's 0.5: each FEC inflicts roughly similar incremental damage, since the dominant cycle mechanisms (mechanical fatigue, fresh-surface SEI repair) do not self-limit the way bulk SEI growth does. LFP in particular is often fitted nearly linear in FEC over wide ranges — one reason its cycle-life numbers are both impressive and comparatively easy to extrapolate.

Semi-empirical cycle ageing law
Qloss,cyc=kcyc(DoD,C,T,SoC‾)⋅FEC z,z≈0.8–1Q_{\mathrm{loss,cyc}} = k_{\mathrm{cyc}}(\mathrm{DoD}, C, T, \overline{\mathrm{SoC}}) \cdot \mathrm{FEC}^{\,z}, \qquad z \approx 0.8\text{–}1Qloss,cyc​=kcyc​(DoD,C,T,SoC)⋅FECz,z≈0.8–1

Cycle-driven capacity loss equals a stress-dependent prefactor times FEC raised to an exponent. The prefactor k_cyc grows with depth of discharge (Wöhler-style, often superlinear in DoD), with charge C-rate, with mean SoC of the window, and has a U-shaped temperature dependence (cold invites plating, heat accelerates chemistry). The exponent sits much nearer 1 than the calendar's 0.5 — LFP fits frequently come out essentially linear in FEC, NMC sometimes mildly sublinear. Practical reading: cycle damage scales roughly with work done, so halving daily throughput roughly halves the cycle-ageing rate — a much more proportional lever than anything on the calendar side.

Superposition: the standard accounting trick

Nearly every engineering lifetime model writes total fade as a plain sum: calendar part plus cycle part. This superposition assumption is popular for honest, practical reasons: it matches how test campaigns are designed (storage tests fit the first term, cycling tests the second), it keeps the regression problem tractable, and it lets you simulate any usage profile by integrating two independent damage accumulators. As a first-order description of fleet data it works surprisingly well — which is exactly why you should know precisely where it stops working before you trust it with a ten-year warranty case.

How test campaigns pull the two apart

Separating the clocks experimentally is conceptually clean and practically fiddly. Storage tests: park cells across a matrix of temperatures and SoCs, interrupt them every few weeks for a check-up (a reference capacity measurement at standard conditions, often plus EIS or a slow cycle for DVA) — this isolates the calendar term. Cycling tests: cycle cells continuously under controlled DoD/C-rate/T with the same periodic check-ups. The subtlety everyone trips over: a cycling test still contains calendar ageing — the cell spends real months at some temperature and some average SoC while cycling, so the raw fade is calendar plus cycle. The cycle term is obtained by subtracting a calendar prediction from the measured total — which means your cycle parameters inherit every error in your calendar model, and the superposition assumption is baked into the dataset before any fitting begins. (Two further gremlins: the check-up itself is throughput and rest, perturbing what it measures; and reversible effects like anode-overhang equilibration can masquerade as capacity loss or recovery in the first weeks.)

🚛Truck reality
The weekend at the depot

Friday 18:00 to Monday 06:00 is ~60 hours of pure calendar time, every single week — over 3 000 hours a year, a third of the year, decided entirely by parking policy. Illustrative numbers in the right ballpark for an NMC pack in a 30 °C summer: parking at 100 % SoC instead of ~50 % can multiply the weekend's calendar fade by a factor of 2–3. Per weekend that is invisible; integrated over five summers it can amount to several percentage points of capacity — comparable to what thousands of kilometres of driving would cost. The fix costs nothing: charge to 50–60 % on Friday, schedule the depot charging finish for Monday 05:30. Smart charging timing is a degradation lever before it is an electricity-price lever.

⚠️Common misconception
"This cell does 3 000 cycles" means nothing by itself

A cycle count without conditions is not a specification — it is marketing. 3 000 cycles at 100 % DoD, 1C, 25 °C is a completely different (and far harder) achievement than 3 000 shallow 10 %-DoD micro-cycles at mid-SoC, which most cells could repeat tens of thousands of times. Always translate to FEC under stated conditions (DoD, C-rate, temperature, SoC window, and the EOL criterion used), and remember the cell was also ageing on the calendar clock during the test. Two datasheets quoting '3 000 cycles' can describe cells whose real-world truck lifetimes differ by a factor of three.
