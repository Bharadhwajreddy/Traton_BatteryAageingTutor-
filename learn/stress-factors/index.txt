11:T487,Why does a fuller battery age faster at rest? Mostly because of where the *anode* sits. As SoC rises, the graphite [[anode potential]] drops toward 0 V vs Li/Li⁺, maximising the driving force for electrolyte reduction and SEI growth. On **[[NMC]]** a second penalty stacks on top — the cathode potential climbs with SoC, accelerating [[electrolyte oxidation]] — so NMC's calendar rate climbs steeply and fairly smoothly with storage SoC. **[[LFP]]** is subtler: its cathode contributes little, so the SoC dependence is anode-only, and graphite's potential is not a smooth ramp — it descends in **plateaus** set by graphite *staging* (the discrete arrangements lithium takes between graphene layers). The anode potential, and hence the SEI growth rate, is nearly constant *within* a plateau and steps when crossing between stages. The practical consequence: LFP calendar stress versus storage SoC looks like a **staircase**, not a slope — moving storage SoC from 65 % to 55 % may change almost nothing, while crossing a stage boundary changes a lot. Good LFP storage guidance is phrased in terms of which plateau to park on, not a precise percentage.12:T44a,High current hurts through three distinct channels. (1) **Heat**: I²R losses raise cell temperature, feeding the Arrhenius factor. (2) **Concentration gradients**: fast (de)lithiation builds lithium gradients inside particles and across the electrode thickness — local overpotentials, inhomogeneous utilisation, and extra mechanical stress, even at moderate average SoC. (3) **The plating gate** — and this one is *charge-direction-only*: only during charging can the anode surface be driven below 0 V vs Li/Li⁺, so charge [[C-rate]] carries a qualitatively dangerous failure mode that discharge C-rate simply does not have. The asymmetry is large: a sustained 2C discharge is mostly a thermal problem; a sustained 2C *charge* is a plating problem with a thermal problem on top. This matters enormously for **[[V2G]]** economics: V2G *discharges* the pack, so per amp-hour it is among the gentlest things a truck battery does — but it is still throughput (FEC accumulate, DoD stress applies) and it reshapes the SoC trajectory and the subsequent recharge. Gentle per Ah is not free per Ah.13:T444,The stress factors above are not independent dials — they are wired together through heat. Current generates I²R heat; heat raises temperature; temperature accelerates ageing; ageing raises resistance R; higher R generates *more* heat at the same power. That is a genuine **positive feedback loop**. In a healthy pack the thermal management system clamps it, but the loop steadily erodes margins: an aged, high-[[impedance]] pack runs hotter for the same duty, ages faster for the same temperature setpoint effort, and demands more cooling energy. The second, sneakier consequence is **spatial**: no thermal system is perfectly uniform, so cells near the coolant inlet live cooler lives than cells near the outlet or in the pack's thermal corners. Different temperatures mean different ageing rates, ageing divergence means resistance divergence, and resistance divergence means the hot cells dissipate even more — [[cell-to-cell variation]] is not just born in the factory, it is *amplified in service*. Since the weakest cell gates the pack, thermal gradient design is lifetime design.14:T416,The workhorse structure of [[semi-empirical model]]s: a reference degradation rate k_ref (measured at reference conditions, where every f equals 1) is scaled by one dimensionless [[stress factor]] per operating condition, multiplied together. Multiplication encodes a strong claim: *separability* — that temperature's acceleration factor is the same regardless of SoC, that DoD's penalty doesn't depend on temperature, and so on. That claim is an assumption, not a finding, and it visibly fails where mechanisms interact: the C-rate penalty depends violently on temperature (plating turns a mild f_C at 25 °C into a brutal one at 0 °C), and the SoC and DoD factors entangle through window placement. Practical models patch this with coupled terms f_{C,T}, piecewise regimes, or plating-specific submodels. The structure survives because it is honest about its data appetite: each factor can be fitted from a one-dimensional sweep of the test matrix. Just never forget that the cross-terms you didn't test are the cross-terms you didn't model.15:T503,Writing a single Arrhenius factor for 'ageing' quietly assumes all mechanisms share one activation energy. They do not. SEI growth, transition-metal dissolution, binder decomposition, and electrolyte oxidation each have their own Ea — and [[lithium plating]] behaves with an effectively *negative* apparent temperature coefficient on the cycling side, since it worsens as T falls. A fitted Ea is therefore an **effective value**: a weighted average over whichever mechanism mix was active in the tested window, valid only inside that window. Extrapolation fails *structurally*, not just parametrically: cool the cell below the tested range during fast charging and the mechanism mix itself changes (plating switches on), so no single-Ea curve — however carefully fitted — can bend the right way. The same logic explains why published activation energies for 'the same' chemistry scatter widely across labs: different test matrices excite different mechanism blends.
