Stress Factors & Electro-Thermal Coupling

A systematic tour of what scales the ageing rate — temperature, storage SoC, window placement, DoD, C-rate — and the feedback loop where heat, resistance, and ageing chase each other's tails.

Temperature: Arrhenius, the 2×-per-10-K rule, and its betrayal

Every chemical side reaction in the cell — SEI growth, electrolyte oxidation, dissolution — is thermally activated, so calendar ageing follows Arrhenius scaling: the rule of thumb is roughly 2× faster per +10 K. Treat the rule as local folklore, not law: the doubling factor depends on the activation energy and on where you are on the temperature axis, and it only describes the chemical part of the story. The betrayal comes on the cycle-ageing side at low temperature: cold slows lithium transport in graphite, so charging cold pushes the anode toward lithium plating — and plating damage grows as temperature falls. Superimpose the two and cycle ageing versus temperature is U-shaped: worst when hot (chemistry) and when cold (plating), gentlest in a band around roughly 15–30 °C. Calendar ageing, with no current flowing, stays monotonic — colder is simply better for a parked cell.

Arrhenius temperature stress factor
fT=exp⁡ ⁣[EaR(1Tref−1T)]f_T = \exp\!\left[ \frac{E_a}{R} \left( \frac{1}{T_{\mathrm{ref}}} - \frac{1}{T} \right) \right]fT​=exp[REa​​(Tref​1​−T1​)]

f_T is the dimensionless factor by which the degradation rate at temperature T exceeds the rate at a reference temperature T_ref (both in kelvin). Ea is the activation energy and R the gas constant. At T = T_ref the factor is exactly 1; above it the exponential takes over. Effective activation energies fitted to full-cell ageing data typically land in the 40–80 kJ/mol range — and Ea ≈ 50–60 kJ/mol near room temperature is what reproduces the folk '2× per 10 K'. Two cautions: the doubling factor itself drifts with absolute temperature (the same Ea gives a different ratio at 0 °C than at 40 °C), and the formula describes thermally activated chemistry only — it cannot and does not capture the low-temperature plating upturn, which must be modelled as a separate, oppositely-signed term.

Try it: Slide the activation energy and watch the acceleration curve steepen. Check where '2× per 10 K' actually holds — then move the reference temperature and see the same Ea give a different doubling factor. Finally, compare a 25 °C depot with a 35 °C one over a truck lifetime: the integrated difference is the point.

Storage SoC: it's the anode potential, mostly

Why does a fuller battery age faster at rest? Mostly because of where the anode sits. As SoC rises, the graphite anode potential drops toward 0 V vs Li/Li⁺, maximising the driving force for electrolyte reduction and SEI growth. On NMC a second penalty stacks on top — the cathode potential climbs with SoC, accelerating electrolyte oxidation — so NMC's calendar rate climbs steeply and fairly smoothly with storage SoC. LFP is subtler: its cathode contributes little, so the SoC dependence is anode-only, and graphite's potential is not a smooth ramp — it descends in plateaus set by graphite staging (the discrete arrangements lithium takes between graphene layers). The anode potential, and hence the SEI growth rate, is nearly constant within a plateau and steps when crossing between stages. The practical consequence: LFP calendar stress versus storage SoC looks like a staircase, not a slope — moving storage SoC from 65 % to 55 % may change almost nothing, while crossing a stage boundary changes a lot. Good LFP storage guidance is phrased in terms of which plateau to park on, not a precise percentage.

SoC window placement: where you cycle, not just how deep

Two cycling profiles with identical 20 % DoD — say 40→60 % versus 80→100 % — are not equally stressful, and the high window loses badly. Three reasons stack up. First, cycling at the top keeps the anode parked near 0 V vs Li/Li⁺ for the entire cycle, so the SEI-growth mechanism runs at its high-SoC rate throughout — you are effectively paying top-SoC calendar rent while also cycling. Second, on NMC the cathode spends the whole cycle at high potential: more oxidation, more particle surface degradation. Third, the plating margin is thinnest at top of charge — the final percent into a nearly full graphite electrode is exactly where overpotentials push the surface below 0 V, so any charging aggressiveness costs most there. Mid-SoC cycling (roughly the 30–70 % neighbourhood) dodges all three penalties, which is why fleet charging strategies that cap routine charging at ~80 % and reserve full charges for long-range days are not superstition — they are mechanism-aware.

Try it: Fix DoD at 20 % and slide the window from 30–50 % up to 80–100 %. Watch the ageing rate climb even though the 'depth' never changes — then switch chemistry and note that LFP cares less, but not zero, and its response moves in steps rather than smoothly.

Depth of discharge: fatigue logic

DoD stress is mechanical at heart. (De)lithiation makes particles breathe; the breathing amplitude scales with the SoC swing, and repeated volume cycling fatigues particles, binder, and contacts — classic LAM. Battery engineers borrowed the formalism wholesale from mechanical fatigue: the Wöhler curve maps cycle depth to achievable cycle count, and fitted DoD stress factors typically come out superlinear — quadratic-ish is a common finding. The consequence is worth internalising: superlinearity means two 50 %-DoD cycles cost less than one 100 %-DoD cycle, even though both move the same total charge (1 FEC). Throughput is not destiny; how you chop the throughput into swings matters. This is also why shallow-cycling applications (grid services, regen-heavy urban driving) get away with astonishing cumulative FEC counts.

C-rate: heat, gradients, and the plating gate

High current hurts through three distinct channels. (1) Heat: I²R losses raise cell temperature, feeding the Arrhenius factor. (2) Concentration gradients: fast (de)lithiation builds lithium gradients inside particles and across the electrode thickness — local overpotentials, inhomogeneous utilisation, and extra mechanical stress, even at moderate average SoC. (3) The plating gate — and this one is charge-direction-only: only during charging can the anode surface be driven below 0 V vs Li/Li⁺, so charge C-rate carries a qualitatively dangerous failure mode that discharge C-rate simply does not have. The asymmetry is large: a sustained 2C discharge is mostly a thermal problem; a sustained 2C charge is a plating problem with a thermal problem on top. This matters enormously for V2G economics: V2G discharges the pack, so per amp-hour it is among the gentlest things a truck battery does — but it is still throughput (FEC accumulate, DoD stress applies) and it reshapes the SoC trajectory and the subsequent recharge. Gentle per Ah is not free per Ah.

Electro-thermal coupling: the loop that bites

The stress factors above are not independent dials — they are wired together through heat. Current generates I²R heat; heat raises temperature; temperature accelerates ageing; ageing raises resistance R; higher R generates more heat at the same power. That is a genuine positive feedback loop. In a healthy pack the thermal management system clamps it, but the loop steadily erodes margins: an aged, high-impedance pack runs hotter for the same duty, ages faster for the same temperature setpoint effort, and demands more cooling energy. The second, sneakier consequence is spatial: no thermal system is perfectly uniform, so cells near the coolant inlet live cooler lives than cells near the outlet or in the pack's thermal corners. Different temperatures mean different ageing rates, ageing divergence means resistance divergence, and resistance divergence means the hot cells dissipate even more — cell-to-cell variation is not just born in the factory, it is amplified in service. Since the weakest cell gates the pack, thermal gradient design is lifetime design.

Multiplicative stress-factor model
k=kref⋅fT(T)⋅fSoC(SoC)⋅fDoD(DoD)⋅fC(C)k = k_{\mathrm{ref}} \cdot f_T(T) \cdot f_{\mathrm{SoC}}(\mathrm{SoC}) \cdot f_{\mathrm{DoD}}(\mathrm{DoD}) \cdot f_C(C)k=kref​⋅fT​(T)⋅fSoC​(SoC)⋅fDoD​(DoD)⋅fC​(C)

The workhorse structure of semi-empirical models: a reference degradation rate k_ref (measured at reference conditions, where every f equals 1) is scaled by one dimensionless stress factor per operating condition, multiplied together. Multiplication encodes a strong claim: separability — that temperature's acceleration factor is the same regardless of SoC, that DoD's penalty doesn't depend on temperature, and so on. That claim is an assumption, not a finding, and it visibly fails where mechanisms interact: the C-rate penalty depends violently on temperature (plating turns a mild f_C at 25 °C into a brutal one at 0 °C), and the SoC and DoD factors entangle through window placement. Practical models patch this with coupled terms f_{C,T}, piecewise regimes, or plating-specific submodels. The structure survives because it is honest about its data appetite: each factor can be fitted from a one-dimensional sweep of the test matrix. Just never forget that the cross-terms you didn't test are the cross-terms you didn't model.

🚛Truck reality
MCS in January

A long-haul tractor rolls into an MCS stop after a cold-soaked night: pack at −5 °C, schedule demanding maximum charge during the mandated 45-minute break. Everything in this section collides right here. Cold graphite means slow intercalation means plating risk, so the BMS derates charge power hard — and the break may no longer buy enough range. The cure is preconditioning: spend energy heating the pack toward ~25 °C before or during the stop. That heating energy is not waste; it is the cheapest battery-life insurance on the vehicle. Fleets that route and schedule winter operations around pack temperature — precondition while driving the last 30 minutes, charge immediately on arrival while the pack is still warm from the highway — get both faster charging and longer life from identical hardware.

⚠️Common misconception
"Cold is good for batteries"

Half true, and the false half is expensive. For storage, yes: calendar chemistry obeys Arrhenius, so a cold parked pack ages slower — a winterised coach fleet stored at 5 °C and mid-SoC is in battery heaven. For charging, cold is the single most dangerous condition in normal operation: it gates lithium plating, the mechanism behind sudden knee points and safety incidents. The same word — 'cold' — flips sign depending on one variable: whether current is flowing. Storage advice and charging advice are different advice, and any rule of thumb that doesn't say which one it is should not be trusted with a truck.
